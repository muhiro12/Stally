# Product Purpose

## Why Stally Exists

Stally exists because personal items often matter through repeated, ordinary
choice rather than through formal inventory.

A coat, notebook, bag, or pair of shoes can become meaningful because it is
chosen again and again. That history is easy to forget. Stally preserves the
signal with one lightweight action: mark the item on the day it was chosen.

## User Problems

### Small Choices Disappear

The user may remember owning an item, but not whether it is still part of
their current life. Stally turns repeated daily choice into a visible record.

### Active Collections Become Noisy

Items that are no longer in regular use can crowd the active list. Stally uses
Archive as a respectful holding place for past favorites, keeping their history
without treating them as current.

### Neglected Items Need Gentle Attention

Some items are added and never marked. Others were once active but have gone
quiet. Stally gathers these into Review so the user can decide whether to keep
trying, archive them, or bring them back.

### Patterns Are Hard To See From Individual Memories

The value of marks grows when they become patterns: active days, streaks,
category concentration, quiet items, weekday rhythm, and monthly movement.
Insights should make those patterns readable without changing the product into
a productivity dashboard.

### Personal Data Needs Safe Transfer

The collection can become meaningful over time. Stally needs a way to export a
complete snapshot, inspect a backup before import, and distinguish safer merge
actions from destructive replace actions.

## Product Philosophy

Stally should feel quiet and cumulative.

The app is not trying to force a habit streak, shame the user for neglecting an
item, or turn a personal collection into an optimization task. The product
language favors "choose," "reach for," "mark," "quiet," "history," "pattern,"
and "nearby."

The intended interaction model is:

1. Add something personally relevant.
2. Mark it when it was chosen.
3. Let the record accumulate softly.
4. Review items that drift.
5. Read the collection as patterns.
6. Protect the record with backups.

## Product Boundaries

The legacy evidence does not establish Stally as:

- A shopping planner.
- A resale tool.
- A social sharing network.
- A wardrobe styling engine.
- A multi-device sync product.
- A general habit tracker.

Backup copy explicitly frames backup files as the user's own archive and
transfer workflow, not as multi-device syncing.

## Emotional Outcome

The user should feel that their collection is understandable and gently cared
for.

Stally should help the user answer questions like:

- What have I actually chosen lately?
- Which items are still waiting for a first real use?
- Which favorites have gone quiet?
- Which archived items might deserve another turn?
- Which categories or rhythms define my recent choices?
- Do I have a safe snapshot before making a risky change?
